The Kartography team represents decades of experience in the humanities and cultural heritage organisations. Kartography's expertise comes from an understanding of how to integrate knowledge systems holistically, with people at its core. It understands the busy and complex environments, the subject areas, and the challenges necessary in helping subject experts evolve new ways of working with technology that augments intellectual processes rather than impedes it.

Data to knowledge transformation

Kartography can model and transform legacy data into a richer and flexible semantic form revealing explicit meaning and relationships hidden in data. This opens up opportunities for expanding the model to meet new processes and questions. This work often involves correcting data issues commonly embedded in traditional database systems. This consultancy is usually made available alongside other knowledge consultancy.

Knowledge Process Consultancy

Kartography can work with you to understand your knowledge processes providing experience and expertise to inform a new knowledge base. Often existing processes have been influenced to service an existing database system. Kartography can help re-evaluate existing processes to inform and align with a new Since a knowledge base system can evolve over time, this work can be informed by longer term strategies or reviews of existing processes which have been determined by a traditional database.

Semantic Experience

Kartography designs interfaces that reflect thinking processes. They can be changed and evolve to reflect changes. Using the ResearchSpace system allow people to generate their own models of information based on changing knowledge. The ResearchSpace system is designed to bring out the meaning in the data rather than impose technical constraints allowing a flexible system that reflects its users thinking.

Knowledge Process Training

Kartography provides standard and bespoke training courses that can help people work effectively with data, regardless of your technical expertise. Kartography teaches non-technical subject experts to understand and make use of semantic link data in their everyday work, and supporting larger scale projects.

Interdisciplinary Humanities & Social Science Consultancy

Kartography draws upon extensive experience of working within Cultural Heritage including the Royal Academy of Arts, the British Museum and the National Gallery. Kartography expertise includes historical and social science methodology and specialisms like Ethnography and Anthopology, Egyptology, Conservation and Conservation Science, and Art History. The Home of ResearchSpace

Kartography consist of the team that designed and developed the ResearchSpace semantic knowledge system at the British Museum. The ResearchSpace system is an open source system based on 10 years of critical research into the foundations of digital research methods in the humanities. It's design is backed by an extensive empirical investigation into the use of computers in historical and social research. Kartography provide custom versions of ResearchSpace without the same overhead as bespoke software because the data holds the meaning and logic not the software which provides different ways to represent and author the data depending on its nature.

Case Studies

The National Archives UK - Collection Care

From Administrative database to Knowledge System

The Collection Care Department (CCD)

The TNA's CCD role is to ensure access to the TNA collections through preservation and display, innovative sector-leading treatment and research, documentation and environmental management. The department had develeoped two key strategy objectives. The first was based on a practitioner-researcher approach in that the traditional division between conservation and science is viewed as anachronistic and progressive development of the department relied on a holistic and integrated approach. Knowledge was derived from all aspects of the departments work.

The Existing System

Like most organisational database systems (for example, collection and conservation) it was considered largely administrative focussing on the basic throughput of conservation work going through the department. In hindsight, it provided very little knowledge about the actual processes, skills, and methods used with the varied items in the TNA collection. There were few benefits in terms of understanding how the department worked and developed. Most of the actual knowledge was spread across different types of repository, mostly documents and spreadsheets, but disconnected and underused. The database had been extended over time but these extensions were pragmatic and not well integrated generating further fragmentation. The increasing gap between what was an essentially static system and the actual workings of the department are typical. Therefore the objective for the TNA project was to create a system that could grow with the department and representing and connecting all the knowledge it generated, creating benefits internally, but also crucially, externally. By reforming this aspect of their work it could underpin their strategy and reach more people.

An Evolutionary Approach

Legacy databases can be difficult and expensive to change because of their technical inflexibility. Semantics and logic are built into the software, not the data, making the system dependent on technicians not invested in the content. The hidden cost is that, in not reflecting the reality of the department’s processes and strategy, it created an overhead. Data went into the system but had no real objective and therefore people were servicing a system which provided little return at any level. However, CCD did not have the time necessary to stop and conduct a detailed review of the department in one major exercise. Using ResearchSpace meant that an evolutionary route was possible. Initially the old data, which was still required, was cleaned and migrated to the CIDOC CRM, creating semantically explicit information in a flexible structure - a graph. Semantic data is higher quality and is more precise but works within a flexible model which can be influenced more easily by subject experts themselves. Once the system was migrated to the new framework it could be developed collaboratively with a common goal to be transparent, educational and better address new challenges. Disruption to the department was kept to a minimum and the system simply continued to change and grow incrementally. The data clean and semantic transformation was completed incrementally over a period of 4 weeks. The system was created from scratch in a similar time span providing a custom system without the usual timescale and resources. Driven from the data, not the software, a custom system could be created without the need for custome support. The Key ingredient was kartography's understanding of the subject area, not just semantic technology.

People

Once a working system was available it was incorporated into the department immediately. This allowed the space for a fuller information exchange about how the department worked, how different sections within the department collaborated and how the department collaborated within the organisation and externally. It also allowed Kartography to describe how the new system operated and the possibilities available to people for its use in supporting the current practices of the department and to improve the system to make it more relevant, gradually introducing the concepts behind semantic data and new ways of thinking about data.

Integration

CCD used the records from the TNA Discovery system which documents the archive using the EAD system. The ResearchSpace system is able to connect with API's and databases and dynamically convert them to a semantic format. A ResearchSpace - Discovery interface was provided that allows CCD to find and import records from Discovery maintaining a link between the two. This also allows CCD to augment the records with material, scientific and historical information including social and historical significant which is highly relevant to preservation and conservation processes.

Evolving the System

A review was undertaken in the context of the CCD strategy and a new version of the system was completed which incorporated an interconnected interface based on a project approach. However, projects are not simply separate standalone pieces of work. Projects are able to be linked allowing past work to inform future work and allow a provenance of methods and techniques that can be traced. Collection items and the activities around them have a life which can now be traced and used to understand change and new developments. Complexity is supported by allowing CCD to break archive items up into components and features from which specific regions and points can be identified and used for precise representation of assessments, analysis, research and treatments that they carry out without resorting to interpretating text fields which previously were the only way to describe details.
